## Design Context

### Users
Business leaders (CEOs, CTOs, founders) and technical decision-makers (engineering leads, architects) evaluating AI partners. They arrive skeptical of hype, time-poor, and looking for signals of real depth. The site must speak credibly to both audiences — business outcomes for leadership, technical substance for engineers — without alienating either.

### Brand Personality
**Confident, quiet, precise.** hoopless.ai doesn't shout. The work speaks. Every word and pixel is intentional. Expertise without arrogance. No filler, no fluff, no buzzwords. The tone is that of a senior engineer explaining something clearly — not selling, not performing, just being direct.

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Tone**: Editorial minimalism with a tactile, considered feel. Think: the inside cover of a well-made notebook, not a SaaS landing page.
- **Theme**: Light mode primary (#FAFBFF lavender-white), dark mode toggle available. The purple (#8B5CF6) is the brand accent — used sparingly for maximum impact, never saturated wall-to-wall.
- **Typography**: Big Daily Short (light serif, display) + Basel Grotesk (geometric sans, body/labels). The light serif weight at large sizes creates authority without heaviness.
- **Motion**: Subtle, document-anchored gradient blobs with cursor repulsion. Scroll reveals sweep from left. Nothing bounces, nothing glows. Motion serves comprehension, not decoration.
- **References**: Orson.ai (editorial warmth, generous whitespace, serif authority), Linear (precision, restraint), Vercel (technical credibility without try-hard energy).
- **Anti-references**: AI hype startups (no glowing gradients, no "powered by AI" badges, no robot imagery, no cyan-on-dark). No big-agency corporate look (no stock handshakes, no blue suits, no buzzword soup). No glassmorphism, no sparklines-as-decoration, no gradient text.

### Design Principles
1. **Restraint is the brand.** Every element must earn its place. If removing something doesn't hurt, it shouldn't be there.
2. **Typography carries the hierarchy.** Size, weight, and whitespace do the work — not color, not icons, not borders.
3. **Purple is the punctuation.** The violet accent appears at moments of intent (CTAs, active states, cursor blob) — never as wallpaper.
4. **Speak to both audiences in one voice.** Copy and visuals should be precise enough for engineers and clear enough for founders. No dumbing down, no showing off.
5. **The gradient system is the signature.** The interactive purple blobs with cursor repulsion are what people remember. Protect this — don't add competing visual systems.
